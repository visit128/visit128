Wine To Waves

Like no other place in the World, California is associated with a certain mentality. It’s a cool, relaxed, innovative place. Highway 128 is exactly that. Stretching from Yolo County in the Sacramento Valley, through the hills and valleys of Napa and Sonoma, to coastal Mendocino, Highway 128 will take you to Michelin Star restaurants, World Class wineries, and California Redwood hiking. Join us on the road that has it all!

The journey west on HWY 128 to the coast begins in Yolo County. Nestled against coastal mountains at the edge of the valley, HWY 128 starts in the historic farming town of Winters, the gateway to your journey from Wine to Waves. Unique shops, restaurants, wineries, and celebrations bring you back to a lifestyle less complicated. From Winters heading west into the Vaca Mountains, the rich farmland, lush with vineyards, fields of crops, and stately orchards, launch your journey into a scenic world. Take your time and cruise through a serene landscape, enjoy the peaceful waters Putah Creek, or the flat waters of Lake Solano through the splendor of the Cold Canyon Reserve and Lake Berryessa. Experience the beauty of the Northern California Blue Ridge Mountains and Berryessa Snow Mountain National Monument. HWY 128 will pave the way.

Historic Sites

Wild grapes certainly grew in abundance in early Napa Valley, but it took settler George Calvert Yount to tap the area's potential for cultivating wine grapes. Yount built one of the homesteads in the area and was the first to plant Napa Valley grapes in 1839.

Charles Krug is credited with establishing Napa Valley's first commercial winery in 1861. His success and leadership sparked a wave of new growth, and by 1889 there were more than 140 wineries in operation, including Schramsberg (founded in 1862), Beringer (1876) and Inglenook (1879). The majority of which can be found along Highway 128. Learn More »

From small family-run wineries to internationally heralded wine houses, Sonoma County nurtures the diversity and passion for winemaking that makes it one of California’s best Wine Country destinations. With 11 state parks and more than 50 regional parks, Sonoma County beckons to anyone with a passion for hiking, biking, fishing, swimming, canoeing, kayaking, horseback riding, bird watching, camping, beachcombing, hang gliding, hot air ballooning, or zip lining. Go To VisitMendocino.Com
Wine
Mendocino County is home to 11 diverse AVAs, from the smallest in the U.S., Cole Ranch at just under one-quarter square mile, to Mendocino Ridge, often dubbed "Islands in the Sky," the only non-contiguous AVA in the U.S. with only vineyards that sit at 1,200 feet or above are included. The region covers more than a quarter-million acres of mountainous land, but just 75 acres of the entire viticultural area are planted. Anderson Valley is now famed for its Alsace varietals and Pinot Noir, both of which are celebrated at festivals every February and May, respectively. Learn More »
Outdoor Activities
With 24 state parks, national forest lands, and the only land-based portion of the entire 1,100-mile California Coastal National Monument, Mendocino County is prime time for hikers. Newly opened in 2016, is additional access to the southern portion of the Lost Coast with its strangely shaped "candelabra trees." The county's rivers, lakes and over 90 miles of unspoiled Pacific coastline provide plenty of opportunities for kayaking, canoeing and boating. Learn More »
Restaurants
Locally raised and grown food is at the heart of Mendocino County's culinary excellence. The first county in the U.S. to ban GMOs, Mendocino County takes food seriously! Foodies delight in chowing down at local restaurants that feature foraged fare, local grass-fed beef, wild-caught seafood and acorn-fed piggies. Diners can take their pick among restaurants offering menus with exquisite vegan cuisine as the star to the season’s best foods prepared in a uniquely refined rustic style. Learn More »
Historic Sites
Much of the village of Mendocino and the Headlands is on the National Register of Historic Places so visitors are immersed in history from the moment they arrive. Another gem in the village is the Temple of Kwan Tai, one of the oldest continuously used Chinese temples in California and the only remaining joss house on the northern California coast. Inland, just outside of Ukiah, is the City of 10,000 Buddhas, home to a large international Buddhist community and sited on one of the state's former mental institutions. In Willits, the Skunk Train, which has run for over 130 years, takes visitors on windy rides up mountains and through the redwoods, or in Fort Bragg along the wildlife corridor of the Noyo River. The Mendocino coast is dotted with unique properties that reflect the character of the area with gingerbread Victorian B&Bs, eco-resorts and ocean-view inns. Farther inland visitors can relax at resorts with hot springs or revel in nature with farm stays. Learn More »
